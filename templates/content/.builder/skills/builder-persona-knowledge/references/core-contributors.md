# Core Contributors (PM/Designer/Marketer)

Product and design ICs who need to ship faster but are blocked by engineering dependency for prototypes and small updates.

## Common Titles

- Senior Product Manager / Product Owner
- Senior UX Designer / Product Designer
- Design Manager
- Marketing Manager / Content Designer

## Recognition Signals

You're talking to this persona when:

- They complain about waiting on engineering for "simple" changes
- They talk about being blocked or dependent on other teams
- They want to prototype or test ideas faster than current process allows
- They mention frustration that shipped product doesn't match their designs

## Priorities

- **Autonomy and creative control.** Want to prototype and ship without waiting in engineering queues.
- **Time on strategic work.** Want research and discovery time. Frustrated when stuck in redlines and implementation details.
- **Work that matches their vision.** Professional pride in seeing designs implemented accurately.

## Metrics They're Measured On

- Feature delivery velocity
- Design quality and user testing insights
- Iteration speed and stakeholder satisfaction

## Pain Points

- **Prototypes take too long.** Waiting on engineering for what they could do quickly. Blocks validation cycles.
- **Small fixes require full dev cycles.** Button text changes sit in sprint queues. Makes team look unresponsive.
- **Prototypes don't feel real enough.** Figma clickthroughs produce unreliable user feedback.
- **Final product doesn't match design.** Multiple redline cycles delay launches and erode trust.
- **Can't visualize product vision.** Stuck describing when they want to show. Blocked when design queue is backed up.

## Why Builder Resonates

- **Full control and faster shipping.** Design and ship changes yourself without engineering bottlenecks. Turn tickets into fixes and improvements directly.
- **Design full interactions.** Break past static design to prototype whole flows and experiences in production code with real components.
- **What you create goes to production.** Engineers review and merge your work. Your designs ship as you intended.

## Discovery Questions

### Open (get them talking)

- "Walk me through what happens when you need a small UI update made."
- "Tell me about the last time you showed your team a Figma design and got feedback that was actually useful."
- "How do you currently validate ideas with users before engineering builds them - and do those prototypes feel real enough?"
- "What's the most frustrating part of waiting for engineers to build something you've already designed?"

*Listen for:*

- **Wait times:** Engineering queues measured in sprints or weeks, requests to shorten the cycle for simple updates
- **Prototype quality:** Don't feel real enough to get quality feedback from technical users, vague feedback on static designs because engineers need interaction
- **Handoff failures:** Final product doesn't match the design they signed off on
- **Autonomy gaps:** Lack of ability to make changes themselves, expensive talent stuck in redlines and heavy PRD work

### Probe for metrics (funnel toward numbers)

- "How long do you typically wait for engineering to build a prototype or make a simple change?"
- "How often does the final implementation match your original design - and how many redline cycles does it take to get there?"
- "What percentage of your time goes to implementation details and spec work vs. discovery and strategy?"
- "How many customer requests or ideas are sitting in your backlog because you don't have engineering capacity to build them?"

*Listen for:*

- **Metrics:** Wait times in weeks or sprints for simple changes, significant time on low-value spec work
- **Quality issues:** Multiple redline cycles where engineers build it wrong despite Figma approval
- **Process bloat:** Heavy PRDs with governance bloat, bar feels higher than it should
- **Missed opportunities:** Backlog of untested ideas, inability to respond quickly to customers or iterate at speed

### Understand their workflow (dependencies and constraints)

- "Who do you depend on to get your work into production or even just to test an idea with users?"
- "When you've tried to prototype or contribute code yourself, what happened - did engineers use it or rebuild from scratch?"
- "What tools do you use today for prototyping - and do those prototypes feel real enough to get good feedback from technical users?"
- "Walk me through your handoff process - what artifacts do you create, and where do things typically break down?"
- "How does your team handle the 'who's allowed to code' question - is there sensitivity around non-engineers prototyping?"

*Listen for:*

- **Dependencies:** Hard dependency on engineering capacity with no ability to self-serve, prototypes getting thrown out while engineers rebuild from scratch
- **Cultural barriers:** Sensitivity about who's allowed to code, concerns about non-engineers contributing code
- **Tool limitations:** Prototypes fail to elicit quality feedback from technical stakeholders, current tools work for concepting but fail to bridge to engineering
- **Waste:** Recreating work in multiple tools, handoff friction where approved designs get built incorrectly

## Common Objections

**"I tried no-code tools before and they didn't work"**
Those tools generated throwaway code outside your codebase. Builder works directly in your real repo using your actual components. The output is production-ready.

**"I don't have time to learn another tool"**
The visual interface is intuitive. Most users are productive in their first session. We'll show you.

**"Engineers won't accept what I build"**
The output goes through your standard review process. Engineers review clean diffs using real components.

## Works With

- **Champions** who they depend on for implementation and code acceptance
- **Influencers** who are their managers and set tool decisions

## Don't Say

- Don't use "no-code" marketing language. They've been burned by overpromises.
- Don't oversell autonomy because they still need Champions to accept their output.
- Don't assume they're technical, so show visual-first workflows.
